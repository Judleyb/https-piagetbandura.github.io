The Theorists

Jean Piaget

1896-1980 • Swiss Psychologist

Cognitive Development Theory

Piaget proposed that children construct knowledge through direct interaction with their environment. His stage theory of cognitive development emphasizes how children's thinking changes qualitatively as they grow.

Key Ideas:
  • Cognitive development occurs in four universal stages
  • Children are active learners who construct knowledge
  • Learning happens through assimilation and accommodation
  • Development precedes learning

Albert Bandura

1925-2021 • Canadian-American Psychologist

Social Learning Theory

Bandura emphasized the importance of observational learning, imitation, and modeling. His social learning theory (later social cognitive theory) highlights how people learn from one another via observation, imitation, and modeling.

Key Ideas:
  • Learning occurs through observation and imitation
  • Behavior is influenced by environment and cognition
  • Reciprocal determinism connects person, behavior, and environment
  • Self-efficacy affects learning and performance

Key Concepts & Visual Representations

Piaget's Cognitive Stages

Four stages of development

The Four Universal Stages
  • Sensorimotor (0-2 years): Learning through senses and actions, object permanence
  • Preoperational (2-7 years): Symbolic thought, egocentrism, language development
  • Concrete Operational (7-11 years): Logical thinking, conservation, concrete problem-solving
  • Formal Operational (12+ years): Abstract thinking, hypothetical reasoning, scientific thought

Piaget identified four stages of cognitive development that all children pass through in the same order, though at different rates.

Observational Learning

Bandura's Bobo Doll Experiment

Learning Through Observation
  • Attention: The learner must pay attention to the model
  • Retention: The learner must remember what was observed
  • Reproduction: The learner must be able to perform the behavior
  • Motivation: The learner must want to copy the behavior

Children learn behaviors by observing others. Bandura's famous Bobo doll experiment demonstrated how aggression could be learned through observation.

Assimilation & Accommodation

How schemas adapt

Adaptation Processes
Assimilation

Incorporating new information into existing schemas

Example: A child calls all four-legged animals "doggie"

Accommodation

Adjusting schemas to fit new information

Example: Learning that cats and dogs are different animals

Schemas are mental structures that organize information. Assimilation and accommodation are the processes by which schemas adapt to new experiences.

Learning Processes

Four processes of observational learning

The Four Processes
1. Attention

Noticing the model's behavior

2. Retention

Remembering the behavior

3. Reproduction

Replicating the behavior

4. Motivation

Having reason to imitate

Attention, retention, reproduction, and motivation are the four key processes in Bandura's observational learning theory.

Jean Piaget: The Cognitive Constructivist

Background & Key Contributions
  • Born: 1896, Neuchâtel, Switzerland
  • Died: 1980
  • Key Theory: Cognitive Development Theory
  • Method: Clinical interviews with children
  • Major Insight: Children think differently than adults
  • Famous Concept: Stages of cognitive development

Albert Bandura: The Social Learning Theorist

Background & Key Contributions
  • Born: 1925, Mundare, Alberta, Canada
  • Died: 2021
  • Key Theory: Social Learning Theory (later Social Cognitive Theory)
  • Method: Experimental studies (Bobo doll)
  • Major Insight: Learning occurs through observation
  • Famous Concept: Observational learning

Bandura's Observational Learning

The Bobo Doll Experiment (1961)

Children observed adults behaving aggressively toward an inflatable doll and imitated the behavior.

Key Finding #1

Children learn aggression through observation

Key Finding #2

Learning can occur without immediate reinforcement

Bandura's Key Concepts

Reciprocal Determinism

Behavior is influenced by and influences personal factors and the environment in a three-way relationship.

Person ↔ Behavior ↔ Environment

Each factor influences and is influenced by the others

Self-Efficacy

Belief in one's ability to succeed in specific situations or accomplish a task.

What is Self-Efficacy?

Confidence in one's capabilities to execute courses of action

Why it Matters

Affects motivation, effort, persistence, and achievement

The Great Debate: Key Differences

Piaget

  • Focus: Internal cognitive structures
  • Process: Individual discovery
  • Role of Social: Minimal
  • Learning Mechanism: Assimilation & Accommodation
  • Development: Stage-based, universal
  • Child's Role: Active scientist

Bandura

  • Focus: External social influences
  • Process: Observational learning
  • Role of Social: Central
  • Learning Mechanism: Modeling & Imitation
  • Development: Continuous, context-dependent
  • Child's Role: Active observer

Nature vs. Nurture • Internal vs. External • Individual vs. Social

Conclusion & Legacy

The Enduring Impact

Both Piaget and Bandura revolutionized our understanding of how children learn and develop.

Piaget's Legacy
  • Stage theory in every psychology textbook
  • Foundation for constructivist education
  • Influenced developmental psychology globally
  • Changed how we view children's thinking
Bandura's Legacy
  • Social learning theory applied worldwide
  • Basis for cognitive-behavioral approaches
  • Influenced education, therapy, and media
  • Explained cultural transmission
